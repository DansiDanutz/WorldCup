## Team History

### The Eagles of Carthage

The Tunisia national football team, known as **Les Aigles de Carthage** (The Eagles of Carthage), carries the weight of being one of Africa's most historically significant football nations. Tunisia was the **first African and Arab team to win a World Cup match**, a groundbreaking achievement that paved the way for African football on the global stage.

Football in Tunisia has deep roots, introduced during the French colonial period. The Tunisian Football Federation was founded in 1957, shortly after independence, and the national team quickly established itself as a continental power. Tunisia has qualified for the **Africa Cup of Nations 22 times** and the **FIFA World Cup seven times** (including 2026).

### 1978: The Trailblazers

Tunisia's World Cup debut in Argentina 1978 was a watershed moment for African football. On 2 June 1978, Tunisia defeated Mexico 3-1 in Rosario, becoming the first African team to win a World Cup match. Goals from Ali Kaabi, Nejib Ghommidh, and Mokhtar Dhouib secured a victory that resonated far beyond football -- it challenged the prevailing assumption that African teams could not compete at the highest level. Tunisia also held defending champions West Germany to a 0-0 draw in the group stage.

That tournament is credited with helping pave the way for increased African representation at the World Cup, eventually leading to Africa receiving more qualifying spots.

### The Group Stage Curse

Despite seven World Cup appearances, Tunisia has **never advanced beyond the group stage**. This is one of the most persistent droughts in World Cup football. Their three World Cup wins -- against Mexico (1978), Panama (2018), and one other -- have never been enough to secure a knockout berth.

In 2022, Tunisia came agonizingly close, beating France 1-0 in their final group match, but results elsewhere meant they finished third in the group and were eliminated.

## Playing Style

Under coach **Sabri Lamouchi**, Tunisia plays a structured, disciplined brand of football:

- **Formation:** Primarily **4-2-3-1**, Lamouchi's preferred setup
- **Defensive solidity:** Built around Montassar Talbi and Dylan Bronn at centre-back, with a double pivot of Ellyes Skhiri and Rani Khedira providing protection
- **Counter-attacking:** With pacey forwards like Elias Achouri and creative talents like Hannibal Mejbri, Tunisia can hurt teams on the break
- **Set pieces:** A significant source of goals, particularly from the towering defenders
- **Tactical flexibility:** Lamouchi has shown willingness to adapt his system based on the opposition
- **European backbone:** 20 of the 26 squad members play in Europe, giving the team a tactical sophistication rare among African nations

## Expectations for 2026

Tunisia faces the toughest group draw of the three teams covered here. Group F features three teams ranked significantly higher:

- **Netherlands:** One of Europe's traditional powers, perennial contenders
- **Japan:** Asia's strongest team, remarkably consistent at World Cups
- **Sweden:** A solid European side with firepower through Alexander Isak and Viktor Gyokeres

**Realistic target:** Simply competing for third place and a best-third-place knockout berth would be a respectable outcome. Tunisia has never escaped the group stage, and this is one of the hardest draws they could have received.

**X-Factor:** **Hannibal Mejbri** -- the 23-year-old midfielder, formerly of Manchester United and now at Burnley, brings Premier League quality and creative energy. If he can unlock defenses against European and Asian opposition, Tunisia might have enough to sneak into the knockout rounds for the first time in their history.
