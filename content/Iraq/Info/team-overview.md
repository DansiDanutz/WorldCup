## Team History

Iraq's football history is one of resilience against extraordinary odds. The Lions of Mesopotamia have produced moments of brilliance on the pitch while navigating decades of war, sanctions, and political turmoil off it.

Their greatest achievement came at the **2007 AFC Asian Cup**, where a war-torn Iraq united behind a team that shocked the continent by winning the tournament. Captained by Younis Mahmoud, who scored the winning header in the final against Saudi Arabia, the victory brought the entire nation to the streets in celebration -- Shia, Sunni, and Kurd together. It remains one of football's most powerful stories of sport transcending conflict.

Iraq's first and only previous World Cup appearance came at **Mexico 1986**, where they lost all three group games (to Paraguay, Belgium, and Mexico). Forty years later, they are back on the world stage in North America, having qualified through a dramatic playoff against Bolivia.

## Playing Style

Under Australian coach Graham Arnold, Iraq blends Western tactical discipline with Middle Eastern flair:

- **Organized Defense:** Arnold has instilled a structured defensive shape, using a back four protected by a disciplined midfield screen
- **Counter-Attacking Speed:** With wingers like Ali Jasim and strikers like Aymen Hussein, Iraq can punish teams on the break
- **Physicality:** The team is athletic and aggressive, not afraid to engage in physical battles
- **Set-Piece Threat:** Iraq is dangerous from corners and free kicks, with tall, powerful players
- **European-Based Core:** Players like Zidane Iqbal (Utrecht), Ali Al-Hamadi (Ipswich), Rebin Sulaka (Port FC), and Merchas Doski (Viktoria Plzen) bring European/international experience

## Expectations for 2026

Iraq faces an incredibly tough Group I with France, Senegal, and Norway. Realistically, advancing from this group is a massive ask:

- **France** is one of the tournament favorites
- **Senegal** is the strongest African team, with elite players across Europe's top leagues
- **Norway** features Erling Haaland and represents a formidable challenge

However, the expanded 48-team format gives Iraq a mathematical chance. A surprise result against Norway or an upset against Senegal, combined with the best third-place team rule, could keep their tournament alive.

The primary goal is to secure Iraq's first-ever World Cup point and potentially their first goal since 1986. Anything beyond that would be a historic achievement for the Lions of Mesopotamia.
