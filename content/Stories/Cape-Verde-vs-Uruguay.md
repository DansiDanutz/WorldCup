# Cape Verde vs Uruguay — The Sharks and the Charruas

*Group H, Matchday 3 — June 22, 2026**

The final matchday of Group H brings a meeting of worlds that should not exist. Cape Verde, a nation of 590,000 people on volcanic islands in the Atlantic, face Uruguay, two-time World Cup champions and the spiritual home of garra charrua — the relentless, never-surrender attitude that defines South American football. Mercedes-Benz Stadium in Atlanta is a sea of blue and sky-blue, the sounds of Cape Verdean morna mixing with Uruguayan candombe in the concourses.

Ryan Mendes, the veteran captain, looks across at Federico Valverde and sees everything Cape Verde is fighting against. Valverde — "El Halcancito," the Real Madrid midfielder with a Champions League medal for every finger — is the product of a footballing culture that has produced Enzo Francescoli, Diego Forlan, and Luis Suarez. Darwin Nunez, "El Pantera," stretches on the sideline with the restless energy of a man who knows that one chance, one moment of chaos, is all he needs. His 13 goals in 38 Uruguay appearances undersell his importance — his runs create space for everyone else.

But Cape Verde did not cross the Atlantic to be star-struck. Their players — Garry Rodrigues, the tricky winger; Logan Costa, the young defender rising in France; Steven Fortes, the veteran centre-back — have spent their careers being underestimated. Every one of them has a story of being told they were too small, too slow, from too small a country. Ryan Mendes has 65+ caps for Cape Verde, each one a rebellion against the idea that only big nations belong on this stage.

In the 81st minute, Nunez receives the ball on the edge of the box. He shoots — the ball rockets toward the top corner, but Mendes, of all people, the 35-year-old forward, has tracked back and throws his body in front of the shot. The deflection loops over the bar. Nunez stares at Mendes. Mendes stares back. There is respect in that look — the respect of warriors who recognize each other.

The World Cup's magic is not in the predictable victories of the powerful. It is in moments like these — when a nation of half a million people refuses to kneel before two-time world champions.
