# Haiti vs Scotland — The Battle of the Underdogs

*Group C, Matchday 3 — June 22, 2026*

It is Matchday 3, and the mathematics are brutal. Brazil and Morocco have likely secured the top two spots in Group C. Haiti and Scotland are fighting for third place — and with the expanded 48-team format, the eight best third-placed teams also advance. For Haiti, a win could mean history: the first-ever knockout stage appearance for the first Caribbean World Cup qualifiers. For Scotland, a win could end a curse that has lasted since 1954 — eight World Cups, zero trips past the group stage.

The stakes could not be higher for two nations that have spent decades as football's nearly-men. Scotland's agony is well documented — unbeaten and eliminated in 1974, the lost goal against Peru in 1978, the Costa Rica upset in 1990. Haiti's is less famous but equally painful — a 52-year gap between World Cups, a nation ravaged by natural disasters and political instability, a football federation that barely exists.

Duckens Nazon carries Haiti's hopes. The striker who was working in a supermarket before football saved him plays with the desperation of a man who knows this might be his only World Cup. Beside him, Derrick Etienne Jr., the midfielder who could have chosen the United States, pulls the creative strings with the technical quality that comes from years in the American and French youth systems.

Scotland's answer is McTominay. The Serie A Player of the Year plays this match with the fury of a man who left Manchester United's bench, conquered Italy, and now stands one win from making Scottish football history. John McGinn, his midfield partner, brings the physicality and craft that have made him a fan favorite at Aston Villa. Andy Robertson, the captain, drives his team forward with the relentless energy that has defined his career.

In the 17th minute, Scotland strikes. Robertson's cross from the left, McTominay arrives at the near post, and his header is powerful and precise. Goal. Scotland leads. The Tartan Army sings so loudly that the stadium trembles. Twenty-eight years of pain, released in a single moment.

Haiti does not fold. Nazon, the supermarket striker, fights for every ball. In the 63rd minute, his persistence pays off. A long ball forward, Nazon holds off Jack Hendry, turns, and shoots. The ball takes a deflection and loops over Gordon. Haiti has equalized. The Caribbean celebration is pure joy — dancing, singing, a nation of eleven million people represented by a single goal.

The final twenty minutes are chaos. Both teams throw everything forward, knowing that a draw eliminates both. McTominay shoots from 25 yards — saved. Nazon breaks clear — Gordon blocks. In the 89th minute, Lawrence Shankland, the Scottish striker, receives the ball in the box, turns, and shoots. The ball hits the post. The rebound falls to McGinn, who slices it wide.

The whistle blows. 1-1. Both teams are eliminated. The Tartan Army sings anyway. The Haitian players collapse on the pitch, then rise, then dance. Because for both nations, simply being here was a victory. And some battles are won just by showing up to fight.
