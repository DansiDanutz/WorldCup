# Iraq vs Norway — Zidane vs the Vikings

*Group I, Matchday 1 — June 12, 2026*

A boy named after a French legend leads Iraq onto the World Cup pitch against a nation of Vikings. If football is a storybook, this chapter was written by a poet with a wicked sense of humor. Stanford Stadium in California is the stage, and the narrative is irresistible: Zidane Iqbal, the 23-year-old Manchester United academy product born in England to an Iraqi-Pakistani family, faces Erling Haaland, the 6'4" goal-scoring machine who has demolished every record in his path.

Iqbal's story is already extraordinary. Named after Zinedine Zidane by his football-obsessed father, he grew up in Manchester, trained alongside Cristiano Ronaldo at United, and chose to represent Iraq — the land of his heritage — over England. He made just one appearance for Manchester United's first team before moving to FC Utrecht for just 850,000 pounds, a fee some United fans called "the steal of the century." His teammate Ali Al-Hamadi shares a similar story — both grew up in England, both chose Iraq, and their bond is visible on the pitch. "We have a special bond because we both grew up in England," Al-Hamadi says. "Zidane is like family."

Haaland and Martin Odegaard represent everything Iraq is fighting against — resources, infrastructure, a footballing culture that produces world-class players with metronomic consistency. Haaland's 120+ goals in 130+ Manchester City appearances, Odegaard's elegant creativity at Arsenal, Sander Berge's midfield steel — this is a Norway team built for the modern game. But Iraq's players carry something that cannot be measured in statistics: the pride of a nation that has endured decades of conflict and still finds joy in football.

In the 23rd minute, Iqbal receives the ball in midfield under pressure from two Norwegian players. He does what he has done since he was a boy at Manchester United — he stays calm, plays a one-two with Amir Al-Ammari, and suddenly Iraq have broken the press. The ball finds Aymen Hussein, who turns and shoots from 20 yards. The ball flies just over the bar. The Iraqi fans — many of whom have traveled from diaspora communities across America — erupt in song. Their team is not here to make up the numbers.

A boy named Zidane against a Viking army. The World Cup writes stories that Hollywood would reject as too improbable. And that is precisely why we watch.
