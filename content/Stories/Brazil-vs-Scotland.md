# Brazil vs Scotland — The Tartan Army Meets the Hexa

*Group C, Matchday 1 — June 12, 2026*

Scotland has waited twenty-eight years for this. Since 1998, when they last appeared at a World Cup and lost to Brazil in the opener, the Tartan Army has endured decades of near-misses, playoff heartbreak, and the slow erosion of hope. Now, in 2026, they are back — and fate, with its cruel sense of humor, has handed them Brazil again.

Andy Robertson, the Liverpool left-back who has won the Premier League and Champions League, leads Scotland out. He is the most decorated Scottish player in a generation, a man who has stood on football's grandest stages and never once flinched. Behind him, Scott McTominay — Serie A Player of the Year, "McTominator," the man whose bicycle kick against Denmark sent Scotland to this World Cup — prepares for the biggest match of his life.

Brazil answers with an embarrassment of riches. Vinicius Junior, the most dangerous winger in the world. Neymar, Brazil's all-time top scorer with 79 goals. Raphinha, the Barcelona winger whose directness is matched only by his joy. Alisson, arguably the best goalkeeper on the planet. Carlo Ancelotti, the most decorated club coach in history, prowling the touchline.

The Tartan Army, 20,000 strong in the stands, sings "Flower of Scotland" with a passion that makes the stadium shake. For eight World Cup appearances, they have never advanced past the group stage. In 1974, they were eliminated despite being unbeaten. The cruelty is embedded in Scotland's football DNA.

In the 15th minute, the moment Scotland feared. Vini Jr. receives the ball on the left, Robertson three yards away. The Scot is one of the few defenders in the world who has faced Vini in the Premier League and survived. He knows what's coming — the stepover, the acceleration, the cut inside. But knowing and stopping are different things. Vini beats him for pace, crosses, and Neymar volleys home. Brazil leads.

Scotland does not crumble. McTominay, the 6'4" midfielder who transformed himself from a Manchester United squad player into Serie A's best, drives forward. In the 39th minute, his long stride carries him past two Brazilian midfielders. He finds John McGinn, whose shot forces a world-class save from Alisson. The Tartan Army roars its approval.

The second half is a siege. Brazil attacks in waves, the samba rhythm building toward an inevitable crescendo. In the 72nd minute, Vini Jr. scores the second — a solo goal that begins on the halfway line and ends with the ball in the net and four Scottish defenders looking at each other in bewilderment. In the 85th, Raphinha adds a third.

Brazil wins 3-0. But Scotland's fans stay long after the whistle, singing, refusing to leave. Twenty-eight years of waiting ended in defeat, but the Tartan Army knows: the tournament isn't over. The group stage has three matches. And Scotland has faced its worst fear and survived.
