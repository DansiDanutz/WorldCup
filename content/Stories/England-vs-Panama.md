# England vs Panama — The Ghost of 2018

*Group L, Matchday 3 — June 21, 2026*

The last time these two nations met at a World Cup, England scored six. It was 2018, in Nizhny Novgorod, and Panama's first-ever World Cup appearance was reduced to a painful lesson in the gap between football's established powers and those still learning the ropes. Eight years later, Panama return with a point to prove and a memory to exorcise.

But this is not the same Panama. Adalberto Carrasquilla, Houston Dynamo's deep-lying playmaker, has become one of MLS's most technically gifted midfielders — a player who dictates tempo with the kind of vision that cannot be taught. Edgar Bárcenas, "El Mago," brings creativity and flair from Liga MX, his low center of gravity and quick feet making him a nightmare for any defender. And Michael Amir Murillo, now at Olympique Marseille, provides explosive pace from right-back that can turn defense into attack in the blink of an eye.

England, meanwhile, carry the weight of expectation that always accompanies them to World Cups. Harry Kane, now at Bayern Munich, still wears the captain's armband with the intensity of a man who knows his time is running out. Jude Bellingham, the Galáctico, has added a World Cup medal to his collection of dreams. And Phil Foden, the boy from Stockport who conquered the world, creates chances that seem to emerge from pure imagination.

The eighth anniversary of that 6-1 defeat hangs in the air. Panama's players have spoken about it all week — not with anger, but with a quiet determination to show that they have grown. Aníbal Godoy, "El Burro" (The Worker), will anchor the midfield with the positional discipline of a man who has spent a career being underestimated. Cecilio Waterman, the veteran forward, knows this might be his last chance to score a World Cup goal — a moment that would define his entire career.

In the forty-third minute, Bárcenas receives the ball on the left wing. He cuts inside — a feint, a shuffle, a burst of acceleration — and Declan Rice is left grasping at air. The shot is struck sweetly, curling toward the far post. The stadium rises, Panama's bench rises, and the ball flies inches wide. Bárcenas looks to the sky. Some moments are perfect in every way except the ending.

This match is about more than revenge or redemption. It is about a small Central American nation refusing to be defined by one painful afternoon in Russia — and proving that football's family is large enough for everyone.
