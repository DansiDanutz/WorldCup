# Croatia vs Panama — The Irresistible Meets the Immovable

*Group L, Matchday 2 — June 17, 2026*

Croatia have reached a World Cup final and a semi-final in the last two tournaments. Panama lost 6-1 to England in their only previous World Cup appearance. On paper, this is a mismatch. But football is not played on paper — it is played on grass, with hearts as much as feet, and Panama have learned the most important lesson in sport: how to get up after being knocked down.

Adalberto Carrasquilla will be the key. The Houston Dynamo playmaker has the vision to unpick defenses and the temperament to remain calm when the world is collapsing around him. His battle with Mateo Kovačić, Manchester City's elegant ball-carrier, will be a study in contrasting styles — Carrasquilla's precision passing against Kovačić's mesmeric dribbling. If Panama's playmaker can match Croatia's midfield, anything becomes possible.

Edgar Bárcenas, "El Mago," is Panama's wildcard. The Liga MX playmaker operates on the left wing but drifts inside like a ghost, creating chances from nothing with quick feet and an imagination that refuses to accept the obvious. Michael Amir Murillo, the Marseille right-back, provides pace and crossing ability that can stretch Croatia's defense.

But Croatia are made of granite. Luka Modrić, still conducting at forty, sees passes that others cannot even imagine. Joško Gvardiol, the Manchester City centre-back, reads the game with an intelligence that makes pace irrelevant. And Dominik Livaković, the penalty specialist, provides the kind of last line of defense that breeds confidence throughout the team.

The fifty-ninth minute produces Panama's moment. Aníbal Godoy wins the ball in midfield with one of his trademark perfectly timed challenges, plays it quickly to Bárcenas, who flicks it over Gvardiol's head with an audacity that makes the Croatian defender smile despite himself. Waterman is through — but Livaković is already off his line, spreading himself wide, and the shot hits his legs and spins away. Sometimes courage is not enough. But sometimes it is everything.

When the final whistle sounds, Panama's players will have earned something more valuable than three points — the respect of a nation that has been to two of the last three World Cup finals. And in Panama City, children will watch the replay of Bárcenas's flick and try to replicate it in the street, because dreams are built from moments like these.
