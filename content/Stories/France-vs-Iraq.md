# France vs Iraq — The Impossible Meeting

*Group I, Matchday 2 — June 17, 2026*

The Stanford Stadium in California hosts a meeting that would have been unthinkable a generation ago. France, the 2018 World Cup champions and 2022 finalists, face Iraq — a nation whose footballing story has been written in the shadow of war, sanctions, and unimaginable hardship. Yet here they are, standing on the same pitch, united by nothing more complicated than a ball and a dream.

Kylian Mbappe, France's captain, adjusts his armband with the casual confidence of a man whose 12 World Cup goals place him among the tournament's all-time greats. Beside him, Michael Olise — "The Magician" — the Bayern Munich winger born in London who chose France over England, Algeria, and Nigeria, brings creativity and flair that makes defenses dizzy. William Saliba, the Arsenal centre-back who calls Mbappe "our leader... not just because of the armband, but because of the standard he sets every day," marshals the defense.

Iraq's story is the tournament's most extraordinary. Zidane Iqbal, the 23-year-old midfielder named after Zinedine Zidane, was born in Manchester to an Iraqi-Pakistani family. He came through Manchester United's academy, trained alongside Cristiano Ronaldo, and now plays for FC Utrecht in the Netherlands. His father chose the name "Zidane" in honor of the French-Algerian legend. Now the son faces the nation of his namesake. "My dad set the bar pretty high," Iqbal once laughed. "I am just trying to be the best Zidane Iqbal I can be."

Aymen Hussein, Iraq's powerful striker, and Ali Al-Hamadi, who also grew up in England, provide the cutting edge. But it is Iqbal who captures the imagination — a boy named after a French hero, now leading Iraq's midfield against France itself. The irony is almost too perfect.

In the 38th minute, Iqbal receives the ball in midfield. Rodri — wait, wrong match. There is no Rodri here. There is Mbappe, bearing down on him. Iqbal, trained at Manchester United, does what he has done since he was a child: he stays calm, plays a simple pass, and keeps Iraq's shape intact. The ball finds Aymen Hussein, who shoots just wide. France breathe. Iraq believe.

The beautiful game writes stories that no scriptwriter would dare invent. A boy named Zidane, leading Iraq against France. Sometimes football's poetry is too perfect to be fiction.
